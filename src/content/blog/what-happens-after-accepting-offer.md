<p><strong>What happens after you accept an offer on your home?</strong><br>
<strong>A:</strong> After accepting an offer on your South Denver Metro home, the typical closing timeline runs about 30 days. That stretch covers disclosures, the inspection period, appraisal, title work, mortgage approval, and a final walkthrough — before you sign papers and hand over the keys. Most of it happens behind the scenes. Here's exactly what to expect at each step, and who's handling what.</p>

<h2 id="what-to-expect">What Should You Expect in the 30 Days After Accepting an Offer?</h2>

<p>Accepting an offer is the beginning, not the finish line. The next 30 days involve real moving parts — some that need your attention, many that run without you.</p>

<p>Without a clear process, this stretch can feel like a black box. But when you know who's handling each step and what to watch for, it becomes a manageable sequence — not a guessing game. My job is to keep you informed at every stage so there are no surprises. That's what this guide is for. Whether you're a <a href="/first-time-homebuyers" style="color: var(--color-accent); font-weight: 600;">first-time buyer</a> or <a href="/first-time-homesellers" style="color: var(--color-accent); font-weight: 600;">first-time seller</a>, understanding the closing timeline and process removes uncertainty and keeps you in control.</p>

<h2 id="day-1-under-contract">What Happens on Day 1 When Your Listing Goes Under Contract?</h2>

<p><strong>What happens immediately:</strong><br>
I share the contract with my team to start processing the transaction, and I update your listing status to Under Contract/Pending on REcolorado MLS. That tells every other agent and buyer — whether they're searching in Highlands Ranch (80126), Littleton (80120), Centennial (80112), or anywhere across the South Denver Metro — that your home is spoken for. It's the official starting gun on the 30-day clock.</p>

<p><strong>A note on backup offers:</strong><br>
We also typically note in the listing that we're accepting backup offers. A backup offer is an agreement where a second interested buyer is next in line if the primary contract terminates for any reason. This matters more than it sounds: if your primary buyer walks during inspection or can't get financing, you already have a qualified backup ready to step in rather than relaunching to market from scratch. It's a layer of protection worth having, and I'll make sure you understand exactly what a backup offer means before we decide whether to accept one.</p>

<p><strong>What you feel:</strong> Relief, usually. This is the moment it becomes real.</p>

<h2 id="disclosures">When Do Disclosures Need to Be Completed? (Days 1–3)</h2>

<p><strong>What happens:</strong><br>
My transaction coordinator takes over the paperwork handoff immediately. They ensure all required seller disclosures are signed by you and delivered to the buyer's agent on time. This is a contractual requirement — delays here can create issues downstream.</p>

<p><strong>What you need to do:</strong><br>
Sign the disclosures when they arrive. Respond quickly — usually within 24 hours. And if anything on the disclosure form is unclear, we help you understand what each one means. You're not on your own figuring this out.</p>

<p><strong>Your stress level:</strong> Low. The coordination is handled. You sign and send.</p>

<h2 id="earnest-money">How Much Is the Earnest Money Deposit? (Days 1–3)</h2>

<p><strong>What is it?</strong><br>
Earnest money is the buyer's show of good faith — typically 1–3% of the purchase price, held in escrow at the title company. It signals the buyer is serious and puts real skin in the game.</p>

<p><strong>What you need to do:</strong> Nothing. The buyer's agent coordinates directly with the title company. You don't touch money at this stage.</p>

<p><strong>Typical ranges:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Move-up homes ($750K+): earnest money starts at $7,500 (1% of purchase price)</li>
  <li>Luxury homes ($1.2M+): earnest money starts at $12,000 (1% of purchase price)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>What if EMD doesn't come in?</strong><br>
If the buyer misses the deposit deadline (usually 3 business days), that's a red flag. I'll flag it immediately and we'll discuss next steps.</p>

<p><strong>Your stress level:</strong> Low. Standard process, handled automatically.</p>

<h2 id="inspection">What Happens During the Home Inspection? (Days 1–10)</h2>

<p><strong>What is it?</strong><br>
The buyer schedules a licensed inspector to physically evaluate your home: roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical, foundation, attic, crawl space — everything. The inspection report becomes the basis for any repair requests.</p>

<p><strong>Your job during inspection: vacate for the appointment.</strong><br>
Plan to be out of the house for the inspection itself — typically 2–4 hours. Buyers and inspectors are more thorough, and conversations are more candid, when the seller isn't present. I'll coordinate the access window with you in advance.</p>

<p><strong>After inspection:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>If inspection is clean: Buyer moves forward. No action needed.</li>
  <li>If inspection finds issues: Buyer sends a written Inspection Objection — a list of items they want repaired or credited.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>How We Handle Inspection Objections</strong></p>

<p>When the objection arrives, I review it with you line by line. We decide together what to repair, what to credit, and what to push back on. I then draft and send the resolution on your behalf.</p>

<p>Your options on any given item:</p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>Repair it.</strong> You hire a contractor. Deal closes clean. Buyer gets what they asked for.</li>
  <li><strong>Credit the buyer.</strong> You reduce the price or provide closing cost credit. Buyer handles the repair after closing.</li>
  <li><strong>Negotiate.</strong> Meet in the middle — fix one item, credit another.</li>
  <li><strong>Decline.</strong> If a demand is unreasonable, we say no. If the buyer walks, the home goes back on market.</li>
</ol>

<p><strong>Real example:</strong><br>
Inspection found a roof with 8 years left. Buyer requested $12K credit. Roof was functional. We countered at $7K. Buyer accepted. Deal closed.</p>

<p><strong>Your stress level:</strong> Medium-High. The inspection period is the most emotionally charged step in the process. The good news: the majority of deals in the South Denver Metro survive inspection — with no objection, a negotiated credit, or a repair agreement. That said, it can be a genuine deal-breaker if buyer demands are unreasonable or if significant undisclosed issues surface. I'll help you navigate both scenarios.</p>

<p><strong>Pro tip:</strong> A pre-listing inspection ($400–600) lets you find problems before the buyer's inspector does. You control the narrative — and surprises become non-events.</p>

<h2 id="title-work">What Does the Title Company Check After You Go Under Contract? (Days 3–5)</h2>

<p><strong>What is it?</strong><br>
The title company pulls the full history of your property: ownership chain, liens, unpaid taxes, HOA fees, easements, and any other claims against the property. The goal is a clean title — so the buyer owns it free and clear.</p>

<p><strong>What the title company is looking for:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Ownership chain (confirming you're the rightful seller)</li>
  <li>Mortgage liens (paid off at closing from your proceeds)</li>
  <li>Property tax liens (paid at closing)</li>
  <li>HOA liens (unpaid dues — also paid at closing)</li>
  <li>Judgment liens (require legal action to clear)</li>
  <li>Easements (utility or neighbor access rights)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>What you need to do:</strong> Nothing. The title company handles all of this from public records and will reach out directly if anything requires your input.</p>

<p><strong>What if there's a problem?</strong><br>
A lien gets paid from your closing proceeds. Example: $5K HOA lien = your net drops by $5K. It's fixable, just reduces your take-home. Judgment liens are rarer and take longer to clear.</p>

<p><strong>Your stress level:</strong> Low. Most titles clear without issue.</p>

<h2 id="appraisal">What Happens if the Appraisal Comes in Low? (Days 5–15)</h2>

<p><strong>What is it?</strong><br>
The buyer's lender orders an independent appraisal to confirm your home is worth what the buyer agreed to pay. Lenders won't lend more than the appraised value.</p>

<p><strong>Your job during appraisal: vacate for the appointment.</strong><br>
Plan to be out for a few hours while the appraiser works through the home — appraisals typically take 1–3 hours. I'll coordinate the appointment window with you in advance.</p>

<p><strong>If appraisal comes in at value:</strong> We proceed to closing. No issues.</p>

<p><strong>If appraisal comes in low:</strong></p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>Renegotiate the price.</strong> Lower the agreed price to match the appraisal.</li>
  <li><strong>Buyer pays the gap.</strong> Buyer covers the difference out of pocket (uncommon but happens in competitive markets).</li>
  <li><strong>Walk away.</strong> Deal breaks. Home goes back on market.</li>
</ol>

<p>Low appraisals are more likely when a home is priced above recent comps. Pricing accurately from the start — within 5% of solid comparables — is the best protection against appraisal surprises.</p>

<p><strong>Your stress level:</strong> Medium. Most appraisals come in at value. Communication solves most gaps when they don't.</p>

<h2 id="mortgage-approval">What Could Affect the Buyer's Mortgage Approval? (Days 5–20)</h2>

<p><strong>What is it?</strong><br>
The buyer's lender verifies income, employment, assets, credit, and property details before issuing final loan approval. Pre-approval is not the same as full approval — this is the full underwriting process.</p>

<p><strong>What can go wrong:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Buyer changes jobs during escrow. Lender pauses review.</li>
  <li>Buyer opens new credit accounts. Debt ratio shifts. Approval at risk.</li>
  <li>Down payment source is unclear. Lender requests documentation.</li>
</ul>

<p>These situations are uncommon — roughly 2% of deals break due to financing — but they happen. I stay in contact with the buyer's agent throughout to catch signals early.</p>

<p><strong>Your stress level:</strong> Low, typically. Stay in the loop through me.</p>

<h2 id="closing-scheduled">How Is the Closing Date Scheduled? (Days 20–25)</h2>

<p><strong>What happens:</strong><br>
Once the major contingencies are resolved — inspection, appraisal, financing — my transaction coordinator coordinates the closing date and time with the title company, the buyer's agent, and you. The location is typically the title company's office, or a mobile notary can come to you — we'll confirm details in advance and make sure all parties have what they need.</p>

<p><strong>What you need to do:</strong> Confirm the closing date and time. Block your calendar. Bring a valid government-issued ID.</p>

<h2 id="final-walkthrough">What Is the Buyer's Final Walkthrough? (Days 25–28)</h2>

<p><strong>What is it?</strong><br>
The buyer does a final walk-through 24–48 hours before closing to confirm:</p>
<ul>
  <li>All agreed repairs were completed</li>
  <li>Included appliances and fixtures are present</li>
  <li>Home is in agreed condition — no new damage</li>
  <li>Any items negotiated to be removed are gone</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>What you need to do:</strong> Have the home accessible and have contractor receipts or photos ready for any repairs you completed. If repairs were agreed to, don't leave this to memory — document everything.</p>

<p><strong>Real example:</strong><br>
Seller agreed to repair a drywall crack. Never followed through. Buyer's final walkthrough caught it. Closing nearly delayed. Seller scrambled same-day for a contractor fix. Two-day delay — and a stressful finish that was completely avoidable.</p>

<p><strong>Your stress level:</strong> Low, if you followed through on what you agreed to.</p>

<h2 id="closing-day">What Happens on Closing Day? (Day 30)</h2>

<p><strong>Closing Disclosure (CD):</strong><br>
Before closing day, you'll receive the Closing Disclosure — the final accounting of the transaction. It shows your net proceeds after your remaining mortgage balance, agent commission, title fees, tax prorations, and any credits to the buyer. Review it carefully before signing. Ask me if anything looks off.</p>

<p><strong>Real example:</strong><br>
A seller expected $530K net. The CD showed $512K. The gap was an $8K HOA lien, $6K in property tax proration, and a $4K commission variance from what they'd estimated. All of it was accurate — but it was a shock because they hadn't reviewed it in advance.</p>

<p><strong>Closing day itself:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Sign the Deed, Closing Disclosure confirmation, and any required HOA or title documents</li>
  <li>Hand over keys, garage openers, remotes, and access codes</li>
  <li>Receive your closing statement confirming net proceeds</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>How you get paid:</strong><br>
The title company receives funds from the buyer's lender, pays off your mortgage and commission, and wires your net proceeds to your bank account. You'll typically receive your proceeds the same day or the next business day.</p>

<p><strong>The closing gift:</strong><br>
I have a closing gift planned for you — a small acknowledgment of the trust you placed in me to get you here.</p>

<h2 id="what-can-go-wrong">What Can Go Wrong Between Accepted Offer and Closing?</h2>

<p><strong>1. Inspection Objections Are Unreasonable</strong><br>
Buyer demands $20K in repairs for cosmetic issues. We review together and negotiate — split costs, offer credits, or push back if warranted.<br>
<strong>Prevention:</strong> Pre-listing inspection. You control the narrative before the buyer's inspector does.</p>

<p><strong>2. Appraisal Comes in Low</strong><br>
Home appraised below the agreed price. Renegotiate, buyer covers the gap in cash, or the deal breaks.<br>
<strong>Prevention:</strong> Price within 5% of strong comps from day one.</p>

<p><strong>3. Financing Falls Through</strong><br>
Buyer's lender denies final approval. Deal breaks.<br>
<strong>Prevention:</strong> Confirm the buyer is fully pre-approved — not just pre-qualified — before accepting. I verify this upfront.</p>

<p><strong>4. Title Issue Found</strong><br>
Unpaid lien or HOA fees surface during the title search. Paid from your proceeds at closing.<br>
<strong>Prevention:</strong> Resolve any known liens before listing.</p>

<p><strong>5. New Damage Found at Final Walkthrough</strong><br>
A pipe bursts. A wall cracks. Seller's responsibility to fix or credit before closing.<br>
<strong>Prevention:</strong> Maintain the home through closing. Don't let anything slide in the final two weeks.</p>

<h2 id="faq">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<p><strong>Q: Can the buyer back out after accepting?</strong><br>
A: Yes — but the consequences depend on when and why. During the inspection period, a buyer can typically walk and get their earnest money back under the Colorado contract. Outside of a valid contingency (inspection, financing, or appraisal), backing out could cost them their earnest money deposit — but only if they've breached the contract terms. The deposit isn't automatically forfeited just because a buyer changes their mind.</p>

<p><strong>Q: Can I back out after accepting?</strong><br>
A: Technically, but the consequences are serious — you could be sued for specific performance (a court ordering you to sell), and you may owe the buyer damages. Don't accept an offer you're not prepared to honor.</p>

<p><strong>Q: Who pays for the appraisal?</strong><br>
A: The buyer pays for the appraisal — it's ordered by their lender but comes out of the buyer's pocket, typically at the time of service or rolled into their <a href="closing-costs-colorado-buyers-2026.html" style="color: var(--color-accent); font-weight: 600;">closing costs</a>. Not you.</p>

<p><strong>Q: Do I have to fix everything in the inspection objection?</strong><br>
A: No. You can offer credits instead of repairs, negotiate the amount, or decline items entirely. If we can't reach agreement, the buyer can walk. I'll help you figure out what's worth conceding and what isn't.</p>

<p><strong>Q: What if the lender is slow and closing gets delayed?</strong><br>
A: Short delays of 2–3 days are common and usually manageable. My transaction coordinator and the title company stay in communication with all parties to catch delays early and minimize disruption.</p>

<p><strong>Q: When do I actually get my money?</strong><br>
A: You'll typically receive your proceeds the same day or the next business day after closing. The title company handles the disbursement — your net proceeds land in your account after the mortgage payoff, commissions, and any credits are settled.</p>
