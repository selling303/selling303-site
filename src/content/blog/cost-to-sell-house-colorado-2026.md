<h2 id="cost-breakdown">What Are All the Costs of Selling a House in Colorado in 2026?</h2>

<p>Selling a house costs money. Not surprise-you-at-closing money, but planned money. Let me walk you through exactly what comes out of your pocket and why.</p>

<p>The biggest misconception? That selling is "free" if you list with an agent. It's not. You're paying for expertise, marketing reach, negotiation skill, and legal compliance — including managing the offer process and a 30-day closing from start to finish. That's not free. If you're in a <a href="../move-up-sellers.html" style="color: var(--color-accent); font-weight: 600;">move-up seller situation</a>, coordinating the sale and purchase timeline adds complexity — understanding your selling costs upfront helps you plan the financial bridge between homes.</p>

<p>Here's what you actually face:</p>

<h3 id="agent-commission">Agent Commission: 5–6% of Sale Price</h3>

<p>This is the elephant in the room, especially post-NAR settlement (2024 onwards).</p>

<p><strong>What changed:</strong> The National Association of Realtors settlement fundamentally shifted how commission is negotiated. Before, seller's agent commission was baked into the MLS listing, and buyer's agents were offered a standard %. Now? The buyer's agent commission is <strong>not</strong> pre-listed. It's negotiated directly with the buyer or their agent.</p>

<p>What does that mean for you?</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Seller's agent:</strong> Typically 2.8–3.2%</li>
  <li><strong>Buyer's agent:</strong> 2.5–2.8% (negotiated, not automatic)</li>
  <li><strong>Total:</strong> 5–6% is common</li>
</ul>

<p>Your agent should be transparent about this split. A great agent earns their commission through smart pricing, strong marketing, and skilled negotiation — and that expertise shows up in your net proceeds.</p>

<p><strong>Cost example:</strong><br>
Home sells for $605,000 (Denver Metro median, Feb 2026, DMAR)</p>
<ul>
  <li>Seller's agent (2.8–3.2%): $16,940–$19,360</li>
  <li>Buyer's agent (2.5–2.8%): $15,125–$16,940</li>
  <li><strong>Total commission: $32,065–$36,300</strong></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>My tip:</strong> Don't negotiate commission as your only leverage. Your leverage is in the <strong>net amount you walk away with</strong>. A great agent at 2.8% who sells your home 5% higher ($28,250 more) nets you far more than saving 0.3% commission. Focus on net proceeds, not commission %.</p>

<h3 id="pre-sale-prep">Pre-Sale Prep Costs: $2,000–$15,000+</h3>

<p>This is where sellers often underestimate. Pre-sale work directly impacts your sale price and days-on-market.</p>

<p><strong>Repairs &amp; Cosmetics:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Paint (interior/exterior): $2,000–$8,000</li>
  <li>Flooring touch-ups (carpet cleaning, polish hardwood): $500–$2,000</li>
  <li>HVAC service/repair: $400–$2,500</li>
  <li>Roof repair: $1,000–$10,000+</li>
  <li>Plumbing/electrical fixes: $300–$3,000</li>
  <li>Drywall/trim work: $500–$2,000</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>When do you do this?</strong> Don't spend $15K fixing everything. Do what kills deals in the Denver metro:</p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>Curb appeal</strong> (landscaping, paint, clean gutters — buyers form opinions before they walk in)</li>
  <li><strong>Functional issues</strong> (heating/AC, water pressure, appliance function)</li>
  <li><strong>Obvious cosmetics</strong> (fresh paint, clean carpets, caulking)</li>
  <li><strong>Staging and presentation</strong> (how the home feels from the moment buyers walk in)</li>
</ol>

<p>Skip: Custom renovations, trendy finishes, ego projects. The buyer will have their own vision.</p>

<p><strong>Staging (Highly recommended):</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Consultation: $300–$800</li>
  <li>Full staging (furniture rental, styling): $1,500–$5,000+</li>
</ul>

<p>According to NAR research, staged homes sell faster and for more money. Jacob highly recommends staging for vacant homes and any home where the furniture or layout might distract buyers from the home's potential.</p>

<p><em>Note: Professional photography is typically covered by your listing agent as part of their services — not an out-of-pocket cost for you. Always confirm this with your agent before signing.</em></p>

<p><strong>Cost example:</strong><br>
A typical pre-sale prep for a $605K South Denver home:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Paint (interior touch-up): $2,500</li>
  <li>Landscaping/curb appeal: $1,000</li>
  <li>HVAC service: $200</li>
  <li><strong>Total: ~$3,700</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3 id="show-ready">Making Your Home Show-Ready: What Buyers Actually Notice</h3>

<p>Buyers in today's market are picky. With more inventory available, they have options — and first impressions matter. Here's what sellers often overlook:</p>

<p><strong>Flooring and Carpet:</strong> You may think 5-year-old carpet is fine. Buyers notice stains, wear patterns, and pet odors immediately. Steam cleaning helps, but if the carpet is visibly worn or pet-damaged, replacing it pays for itself in offers. Similarly, scuffed hardwood floors can be re-coated affordably — and the difference in perceived value is significant.</p>

<p><strong>Paint:</strong> Sellers often say "why paint if the buyer will change the color?" Here's the answer: fresh paint makes rooms feel brighter, cleaner, and larger. Neutral tones with clean trim eliminate the objection before buyers can raise it. A freshly painted home photographs better, shows better, and sells faster. It's one of the highest-ROI pre-sale investments you can make.</p>

<p><strong>Declutter and depersonalize:</strong> Buyers need to see themselves in the space. Pack away family photos, excess furniture, and personal items. Less is more. A decluttered home photographs better and feels more spacious.</p>

<p><strong>Deep clean everything:</strong> Kitchens and bathrooms especially. If it smells or looks dirty, buyers mentally deduct — and they deduct more than the actual cleaning cost.</p>

<h3 id="taxes-inspections">What About Taxes &amp; Inspections?</h3>

<p><strong>Home Inspection:</strong> Buyer typically pays (~$400–$600). You don't.</p>

<p><strong>Capital Gains Tax:</strong> Consult your CPA, but primary residence exclusions are generous ($250K single, $500K married). Colorado has no state capital gains tax on real estate.</p>

<p><strong>Property Tax:</strong> You pay through closing day (prorated). Buyer pays from closing forward.</p>

<h2 id="where-lose-money">Where Do Denver Metro Sellers Lose the Most Money?</h2>

<p>Not in commission or closing costs. In these three places:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Overpricing.</strong> Asking $625K when the market supports $605K costs you $20K+ immediately, plus 30+ extra <a href="why-homes-sit-on-market-south-denver.html">days on market in South Denver</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Bad photos/online presence.</strong> Homes are bought on photos first. A $500 professional photo shoot vs. iPhone photos? That's $10–20K difference in offers.</li>
  <li><strong>Waiting for "perfect timing."</strong> Carrying costs (property tax, insurance, utilities, opportunity cost) of waiting 6 months can exceed $10K.</li>
</ol>

<p><strong>The fix:</strong> Price right, photograph well, market honestly. Everything else is details.</p>

<h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>

<p><strong>Q: Can I negotiate the agent commission?</strong><br>
A: Yes. But negotiate based on the service and value, not just the %. If an agent is earning their keep through smart pricing and strong marketing, 2.8% beats 2.3% with an agent who underperforms.</p>

<p><strong>Q: Who pays the buyer's agent commission?</strong><br>
A: Post-NAR settlement, the seller typically pays the buyer's agent commission directly out of proceeds, as outlined in the listing agreement and purchase contract. The exact structure depends on how the contract is written and negotiated. Your agent should walk you through this clearly before you list.</p>

<p><strong>Q: Do I have to pay to fix everything the inspector finds?</strong><br>
A: No. You can negotiate repairs, offer a credit, or let the buyer walk and buy elsewhere. It's negotiation, not obligation. <a href="what-happens-after-accepting-offer.html" style="color: var(--color-accent); font-weight: 600;">Read more about the inspection period.</a></p>
