<p>If you own a home anywhere in the South Denver Metro — from <a href="/neighborhoods/englewood">Englewood</a> to <a href="/neighborhoods/castle-pines">Castle Pines</a> — the 2026 watering season looks different from last year. Denver Water declared Stage 1 drought on March 25, <a href="/neighborhoods/highlands-ranch">Highlands Ranch</a> followed with its own Stage 1 declaration the same week, and snowpack in the basin feeding <a href="/neighborhoods/highlands-ranch">Highlands Ranch</a> and <a href="/neighborhoods/centennial">Centennial</a> sits at roughly 38% of normal.</p>

<p>The challenge for homeowners: every water district in the metro operates on its own schedule, with its own rules, its own enforcement, and its own penalties. Finding consolidated information is surprisingly difficult — Jacob Stark searched for it himself and came up empty, which is why this guide exists.</p>

<p>Below is the district-by-district breakdown for every community in the South Denver suburbs, along with smart watering strategies to keep your lawn alive on a restricted schedule.</p>

<h2 id="denver-water">What Are Denver Water's 2026 Drought Restrictions? (Littleton, Centennial, Lone Tree)</h2>

<p><a href="https://www.denverwater.org/residential/rebates-and-conservation-tips/summer-watering-rules" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Denver Water</a> serves a large portion of the South Denver Metro, including most of Littleton, Centennial, and Lone Tree. Under the Stage 1 drought declaration, watering is restricted to <strong>two assigned days per week</strong>:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Even addresses:</strong> Sunday and Thursday</li>
<li><strong>Odd addresses:</strong> Wednesday and Saturday</li>
<li><strong>Commercial and multifamily:</strong> Tuesday and Friday</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>No watering between 10am and 6pm.</strong> Denver Water also recommends waiting until mid-to-late May to turn on sprinkler systems at all — late spring freezes can damage irrigation lines and waste water before lawns are actively growing.</p>

<p>The most notable restriction for homeowners: <strong>new sod installations are strongly discouraged and effectively impractical</strong> under Stage 1. New turf requires daily watering to establish, which isn't possible on a two-day-per-week schedule. If you're planning curb appeal improvements ahead of a home sale, traditional sod is essentially off the table in Denver Water's service area. The district's goal is a 20% reduction in outdoor water use across its service territory.</p>

<h3>Denver Water's New Drought Surcharges (Effective May 2026)</h3>

<p>On April 8, 2026, Denver Water's board approved <strong>temporary drought pricing</strong> — the first time the utility has imposed drought surcharges in over 20 years (the last time was during the 2002–2004 drought). The surcharges take effect with May water use, which means they'll first appear on June bills, and run through April 30, 2027.</p>

<p>The pricing targets outdoor water use specifically:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Tier 1 (essential indoor use):</strong> Exempt — no drought surcharge on indoor water.</li>
<li><strong>Tier 2 (outdoor use up to 15,000 gallons/month above Tier 1):</strong> +$1.10 per 1,000 gallons on top of standard 2026 rates.</li>
<li><strong>Tier 3 (heavy outdoor use above Tier 2 threshold):</strong> +$2.20 per 1,000 gallons on top of standard 2026 rates.</li>
</ul>

<p>What does this mean in dollars? Denver Water estimates that a suburban customer (Littleton, Centennial, Lone Tree) who doesn't conserve will see annual water costs rise from roughly $877 to $929. High-use households — think large irrigated lots — could go from $1,050 to $1,126 annually. Inside the city of Denver, the numbers are lower but still meaningful: non-conservers from $684 to $729, and high users from $803 to $879.</p>

<p>The takeaway for South Denver homeowners: watering restrictions now have a direct financial cost beyond fines. Overwatering doesn't just risk a warning from Denver Water — it hits your bill at $1.10 to $2.20 per thousand gallons above your indoor baseline.</p>

<h2 id="highlands-ranch">What Are the Highlands Ranch Watering Rules for 2026?</h2>

<p>The <a href="https://www.douglascountynewspress.com/news/article_20de4eb0-8819-44fa-8c59-c31350d26d4c.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Highlands Ranch Metro District</a> enacted its own Stage 1 drought restrictions in late March 2026. Snowpack in the Highlands Ranch basin measured just 38%, and demand was running 23% above predicted levels — a combination that triggered mandatory limits.</p>

<p>The Highlands Ranch watering schedule:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Odd addresses:</strong> Wednesday and Saturday</li>
<li><strong>Even addresses:</strong> Thursday and Sunday</li>
<li><strong>Multi-family and apartments:</strong> Monday and Friday</li>
<li><strong>Non-residential properties:</strong> Tuesday and Sunday</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>No watering between 10am and 6pm, May 1 through September 30.</strong> The district is targeting a 15–20% reduction in outdoor water use. Highlands Ranch homeowners should check with the <a href="https://www.highlandsranchwater.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Highlands Ranch Metropolitan District</a> for updates as conditions evolve through summer.</p>

<h2 id="parker">What's Parker Water & Sanitation District's 2026 Schedule?</h2>

<p><a href="https://pwsd.org/3366/Watering-Restrictions" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Parker Water & Sanitation District</a> enforces year-round watering efficiency rules, though Parker has not declared a Stage 1 drought as of early April 2026. The key rules:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>No watering between 10am and 6pm</strong></li>
<li><strong>Water waste is prohibited</strong> — runoff onto sidewalks, streets, or adjacent properties; broken or misdirected sprinkler heads; and unattended hose watering all violate district rules</li>
</ul>

<p>Parker homeowners have a resource most districts don't offer: <strong>free sprinkler system consultations</strong> through Parker Water's partnership with <a href="https://resourcecentral.org/sprinklers/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Resource Central's Slow the Flow program</a>. A certified technician inspects your irrigation system, identifies waste, and provides a customized watering schedule for your property. Appointments are first-come, first-served during the irrigation season (typically June through August) until funding runs out — so <a href="https://pwsd.org/874/Water-Efficiency-Conservation" target="_blank" rel="noopener">sign up early through Parker Water's conservation page</a> or contact <a href="mailto:conservation@pwsd.org">conservation@pwsd.org</a>.</p>

<h2 id="castle-rock">How Does Castle Rock's Water Budget System Work?</h2>

<p>Castle Rock takes a fundamentally different approach to water management. Instead of restricting watering to specific days, <a href="https://crconserve.com/158/Watering-Schedule" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Castle Rock Water</a> assigns each property a <strong>water budget</strong> — a monthly allocation based on your lot size, irrigated area, and weather conditions.</p>

<p>The mandatory year-round watering schedule limits irrigation to <strong>every third day</strong>, determined by the symbol on your address (square, circle, or diamond). Watering is allowed <strong>before 8am or after 8pm only</strong>, and the irrigation season runs May 1 through September 30.</p>

<p>Where Castle Rock gets serious is the budget enforcement. Exceed your monthly water budget and surcharges kick in:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Tier 1 overage:</strong> $6.91 per 1,000 gallons</li>
<li><strong>Tier 2 overage:</strong> $10.31 per 1,000 gallons</li>
</ul>

<p>Castle Rock has not declared drought restrictions for 2026 — reservoir storage is actually at record highs after Reservoir 2 came online — but the Town Council is considering a voluntary Stage 1 recommendation (10% reduction). Even without a drought declaration, the budget system and surcharges make Castle Rock one of the most financially consequential districts for overwatering.</p>

<h2 id="castle-pines">What About Castle Pines Watering Schedules in 2026?</h2>

<p>The <a href="https://www.cpnmd.org/water" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Castle Pines North Metropolitan District</a> uses an address-based watering schedule where the <strong>last digit of your address</strong> determines your assigned watering day. The specific 2026 schedule had not been published at the time of this writing.</p>

<p>Castle Pines homeowners should check the CPNMD website directly for the updated 2026 schedule when it's released. Given the drought conditions affecting neighboring districts, Castle Pines restrictions may tighten as summer approaches.</p>

<h2 id="englewood">What Are Englewood's Current Watering Guidelines?</h2>

<p>The <a href="https://www.englewoodco.gov/government/city-departments/utilities/customers/watering-schedule" target="_blank" rel="noopener">City of Englewood</a> operates its own water utility and has not imposed mandatory drought restrictions as of early April 2026. Instead, the City is asking residents to <strong>voluntarily follow a three-day-per-week watering schedule</strong> aligned with Denver Water's broader metro-wide conservation push.</p>

<p>The City's standing guidelines recommend:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Water only before 10:00am or after 6:00pm</strong></li>
<li><strong>Water no more than three days per week</strong></li>
<li><strong>Water only when your lawn or garden actually needs it</strong> — not on a fixed autopilot schedule</li>
</ul>

<p>Englewood has stated that if mandatory restrictions become necessary, they'll notify customers via the City website. Given the regional drought severity, Englewood homeowners should plan for the possibility that voluntary guidelines could become mandatory rules as summer progresses.</p>

<h2 id="smart-watering">How Do You Maintain a Healthy Colorado Lawn on Fewer Watering Days?</h2>

<p>Fewer watering days doesn't have to mean a brown lawn. The key is shifting from frequent, shallow watering to deep, infrequent irrigation. According to <a href="https://extension.colostate.edu/resource/lawn-care-basics/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Colorado State University Extension's Lawn Care Basics guide</a>, this approach actually produces healthier turf with deeper root systems. CSU Extension also publishes a detailed <a href="https://extension.colostate.edu/resource/methods-to-schedule-home-lawn-irrigation/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">home lawn irrigation scheduling guide</a> for homeowners who want to dial in their system precisely.</p>

<h3>Water Deep, Not Often</h3>

<p>Each watering session should wet the soil to 3–5 inches deep. Two deep watering days per week grows stronger roots than daily light sprinkles. Frequent shallow watering creates shallow root systems that are more vulnerable to heat stress and disease — exactly what you don't want during a drought.</p>

<h3>Use the Cycle-and-Soak Method</h3>

<p>Instead of running each sprinkler zone for one long session, divide the total watering time into two or three shorter cycles with 30–60 minute rest periods between each. This prevents runoff (which violates district rules and wastes water) and allows deeper soil penetration. Colorado's clay-heavy soils are especially prone to runoff, making <a href="https://extension.colostate.edu/topic-areas/yard-garden/watering-established-lawns-7-199/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cycle-and-soak</a> the single most effective technique for South Denver lawns.</p>

<p><strong>How to set it up:</strong> If a zone normally runs for 24 minutes, program three 8-minute cycles with a one-hour soak between each — so the zone runs at, say, 4:00am, 5:00am, and 6:00am. Most modern irrigation controllers have a "cycle and soak" or "multiple start times" feature built in. On a pop-up spray head system with clay soil, CSU Extension recommends roughly 8–10 minutes per cycle to apply about a quarter inch of water per pass, with two or three passes per watering day.</p>

<h3>Know Your Lawn's Actual Water Needs</h3>

<p>Kentucky bluegrass — the <a href="https://planttalk.colostate.edu/topics/lawns/1521-choosing-lawn-grass-colorado/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">most widely used lawn grass in Colorado</a> — needs about 1 inch of water per week in April and May, 1.25 inches in June, and 1.5 inches in July. During hot, dry, windy stretches, demand can spike to 2.25 inches per week. Place a tuna can or rain gauge on your lawn during a watering cycle to measure actual output and calibrate your system accordingly.</p>

<h3>Mow Higher</h3>

<p>Keep your mower at 2.5 to 3 inches for all Colorado grass species. Taller grass shades the soil, reduces evaporation, and retains moisture longer between watering days. This is the simplest, cheapest thing any homeowner can do to stretch their water allocation further.</p>

<h2 id="real-estate">How Do Watering Restrictions Affect Home Sellers and Buyers in South Denver?</h2>

<p>Watering restrictions aren't just a lawn care issue — they have real implications for real estate transactions across the South Denver Metro.</p>

<h3>For Sellers: Curb Appeal Under Constraints</h3>

<p>A green, well-maintained lawn is still one of the first things buyers notice. Under drought restrictions, sellers need to plan ahead. In Denver Water's service area, where new sod is effectively impractical under the two-day watering limit, <a href="/blog/7-smartest-home-upgrades-before-selling-2026">drought-tolerant landscaping or xeriscaping</a> becomes the smart curb appeal play. Native grasses, rock beds, and low-water plantings can look intentional and attractive — and they signal to buyers that the home's outdoor maintenance costs will be lower long-term.</p>

<h3>For Buyers: Factor Water Costs Into Your Budget</h3>

<p>Water costs are no longer a background line item — they're a real budget factor in multiple South Denver districts. In Castle Rock, water budget surcharges can meaningfully affect your monthly costs, especially if the property has a large irrigated lot. And now in Denver Water's service area (Littleton, Centennial, Lone Tree), the new drought surcharges mean a suburban home with heavy outdoor irrigation could cost $1,050–$1,126 annually for water — up from $877–$1,050 before drought pricing. Ask for 12 months of water bills during due diligence regardless of which district you're buying in. In Englewood, where mandatory restrictions could follow the current voluntary guidelines, it's worth considering how a property's landscaping would hold up under tighter rules.</p>

<h3>For Relocation Buyers: Colorado Water Is Different</h3>

<p>Out-of-state buyers are consistently surprised by Colorado's water rules. If you're <a href="/relocation">relocating to the South Denver Metro</a>, understanding your new district's watering schedule before you close is important. HOA enforcement of watering schedules can result in fines, and the patchwork of district rules means your watering day in <a href="/neighborhoods/lone-tree">Lone Tree</a> is different from your neighbor's in unincorporated Douglas County.</p>
