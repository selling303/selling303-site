What Happens After You Accept an Offer on Your Home?

Typically 30 days. Here's exactly what happens each week — and what can go sideways.

Jacob Stark March 28, 2026 · 12 min read

Q: What's the timeline after you accept an offer to closing?
A: Typically 30 days. Day 1: earnest money deposit. Days 1–10: inspection period (buyer inspects, negotiates repairs/credits). Days 11–25: appraisal, title work, mortgage approval. Days 26–30: final walkthrough, closing disclosure signed, closing day. You're done.

Why This Matters (And Why Uncertainty Costs You Peace)

You just accepted an offer. Great. Now what?

The next 30 days feel long when you don't know what's coming. Uncertainty is stressful. You don't know if the inspection will kill the deal. You don't know if the appraisal will come in. You don't know if closing will be on time.

Let me walk you through exactly what happens, when it happens, and what could go sideways.

Days 1–2: Earnest Money Deposit (EMD)

What is it?
Earnest money is the buyer's show of good faith. It's typically 1–3% of the purchase price, held in escrow by a title company or attorney. It proves the buyer is serious.

What you need to do:
Nothing. The buyer's real estate agent coordinates this directly with the title company. You don't handle money at this stage.

How much? Depends on the offer. Typical range:

  • Starter homes ($300–450K): $3,000–9,000 EMD
  • Mid-range homes ($450–600K): $6,000–15,000 EMD
  • Higher-end homes ($600K+): $15,000–30,000+ EMD

What if it doesn't come in?
If the buyer doesn't deposit earnest money within the timeframe (usually 3 days), that's a red flag. Discuss with your agent. It may indicate financing issues or a flaky buyer.

Your stress level: Low. This is standard. Happens automatically.

Days 1–10: Inspection Period (The Big One)

What is it?
The buyer (or their inspector) physically inspects your home for major defects. They're looking for structural issues, roof problems, HVAC function, plumbing, electrical, foundation cracks, water damage, etc.

Timeline:
Typically 7–10 days after acceptance. Buyer schedules with an inspector. You get notice (usually 24 hours). Inspector shows up for 2–4 hours.

What happens during inspection:

  • Inspector crawls attic, checks furnace, tests AC, runs water, checks electrical panels, looks for cracks, photos everything.
  • You can be home or away (your choice, usually away is easier).
  • Takes 2–4 hours depending on home size.

What happens after inspection:

  • If inspection is clean: Buyer says "looks good, moving forward." Easy.
  • If inspection finds issues: Buyer sends you a list of "inspection objections" — things they want repaired or credits for.

Inspection Objections: What You're Facing

This is where deals get tense.

Let's say the inspector finds: HVAC needing service ($200), roof with 5 years left (not 10) ($8,000 roof credit), drywall crack (cosmetic) ($500), water stain in basement (needs investigation) (unknown cost).

Buyer sends you a list: "Please repair HVAC, replace roof, repair drywall, or provide credits."

Your options:

  1. Repair it. You pay contractor to fix. Closes the deal cleanly.
  2. Credit the buyer. You provide a lump sum at closing. Buyer fixes it (or doesn't).
  3. Negotiate. Split the difference. You fix one thing, credit the other.
  4. Walk away. If repair cost is massive and unreasonable, renegotiate or let buyer walk.

Real example from my recent deal:
Inspection found old roof (10+ years). Buyer wanted $12K credit. Roof was functional but aging. We negotiated: seller credits $7K, buyer accepts risk that roof lasts 2–3 more years. Deal closed.

Your stress level: Medium-High. This is where deals sometimes break. But most survive.

Pro tip: I recommend a pre-listing inspection before you list (costs $400–600). Find problems before the buyer's inspector does. You control the narrative. "We already know about that, here's the quote to fix it" is better than a surprise in inspection objections.

Days 3–5: Title Work Begins (Background Check for Your Home)

What is it?
Title company pulls the history of your property ownership, checks for liens, unpaid taxes, easements, or other claims against the property. Ensures the title is "clean" so the buyer can own it free and clear.

What they're looking for:

  • Previous owner chains (you actually own it)
  • Property tax liens (you paid taxes)
  • Mortgage liens (your lender has a claim; gets paid at closing)
  • HOA liens (you paid HOA fees)
  • Judgment liens (lawsuits against you or previous owners)
  • Easements (utility companies, neighbors with rights to your land)

What you need to do:
Nothing. Title company handles all of this. They pull public records.

What if there's a problem?
If a lien is found, you need to pay it off at closing. Your proceeds get reduced. Example: $5K property tax lien = title company pays it from your closing proceeds. You get $5K less.

Unpaid HOA fees are common and fixable (paid at closing). Judgment liens are rarer but serious (require court involvement to clear).

Your stress level: Low. Most homes clear title easily.

Days 5–15: Appraisal (Does the Home's Value Match the Offer?)

What is it?
The buyer's lender orders an appraisal — an independent professional assesses your home's market value to ensure it matches the offer price. Lenders won't lend more than the home's worth.

Why it matters:
Let's say you agreed to sell for $565K, but the appraisal comes back at $545K. The lender won't lend $450K (their 80% LTV) against a $545K value. Buyer is short $20K. Deal can break here.

What if appraisal is low? Options:

  1. Renegotiate price down. Buyer's lender won't fund at $565K for a $545K home. Price drops to $545K (or buyer pays gap in cash).
  2. Buyer adds cash. Buyer puts in more down payment to cover the gap.
  3. Walk away. Deal breaks. Home goes back on market.

Low appraisals are more common in slower markets. Right now (Feb 2026), with 55-day DOM, appraisals can miss expectations.

Your stress level: Medium. This can tank deals, but communication usually saves them.

Pro tip: Price your home within 5% of comps from day one. If you're priced right, appraisal won't be a surprise.

Days 5–20: Mortgage Approval (The Financing Handshake)

What is it?
Buyer's lender processes the mortgage application, verifies income, employment, assets, credit, and property details. They decide if the buyer qualifies for the loan amount promised.

What can go wrong:

  • Buyer's employment changes. Lender pauses approval.
  • Buyer opens new credit card or car loan. Debt ratio changes. Approval at risk.
  • Buyer's down payment source is unclear. Lender requests proof of funds.
  • Buyer's income is misrepresented. Approval denied.

Rare, but happens. ~2% of deals break due to financing issues.

Your stress level: Low, usually. But keep your agent in the loop. If there are financing concerns, address them now (not closing day).

Days 25–28: Final Walkthrough (Last Chance to Check)

What is it?
Buyer (or buyer's agent) does a final walk-through of the home 24–48 hours before closing to confirm:

  • All agreed repairs were completed
  • Appliances/fixtures agreed to stay are present
  • Home is in agreed condition (no new damage)
  • Agreed items have been removed (if buyer negotiated seller removing something)

What you need to do:

  • Be out or available (agent coordinates access).
  • Have home accessible and empty-ish.
  • If repairs were done, have contractor proof available (receipt, photo, warranty).

Real example:
Seller agreed to repair drywall crack. Inspection showed crack. Seller never fixed it. Buyer's final walkthrough found it. Deal nearly broke. Seller scrambled to get contractor in and fix same day. Closing delayed 2 days.

Your stress level: Low, if you've been honest and followed through on repairs.

Days 28–30: Closing Disclosure & Closing Day

Closing Disclosure (CD) is the final loan document. It shows:

  • Loan amount and interest rate
  • Monthly payment
  • Closing costs (buyer's and seller's)
  • Your final net proceeds (what you walk away with)

Review it carefully. Verify all costs are accurate. Check your net proceeds. Ask your agent to explain anything unclear.

Real example:
Seller expected $530K net. CD showed $512K. Why? $8K HOA lien (unpaid fees), $6K property tax proration, $4K commission variance. Once explained, seller understood. But they were surprised because they hadn't reviewed it.

Closing Day: Final legal documents signed. Funds transfer. Title transfers. You're done.

What do you do?

  • Sign final papers (Deed, Closing Disclosure confirmation, HOA disclosures, etc.)
  • Hand over keys, garage openers, remotes, codes
  • Confirm final walkthrough was acceptable
  • Receive closing statement showing net proceeds

How do you get paid?
Closing attorney/title company receives funds from buyer's lender, pays off your mortgage lender and agent commission, then wires remaining balance to your bank account. Usually within 1–3 business days after closing.

What Can Actually Go Wrong (And How to Handle It)

1. Inspection Objections Are Unreasonable
Buyer demands $20K in repairs for cosmetic issues. Negotiate. Split costs. Offer credit. If buyer is truly unreasonable, you can walk away.
Fix: Have pre-listing inspection. Control the narrative.

2. Appraisal Comes in Low
Home appraised at $545K but you agreed to $565K. Renegotiate price or buyer adds cash.
Fix: Price right from the start. Within 5% of comps = appraisal won't surprise you.

3. Financing Falls Through
Buyer's lender denies approval at the last minute. Deal breaks.
Fix: Confirm buyer is pre-approved from day 1. No surprises.

4. Title Issue Discovered
Lien found. HOA fees unpaid. You have to pay it off before closing.
Fix: Settle any liens before listing. Clean title from start.

5. Buyer Finds New Damage at Final Walkthrough
Pipe bursts. New wall crack. Seller responsibility to fix or credit.
Fix: Don't be careless. Maintain home through closing period.

Your Peace of Mind Checklist

After you accept an offer, do this:

  • Day 1: Confirm earnest money deposit timing with your agent
  • Day 3: Get a pre-listing inspection if you haven't already (find problems early)
  • Day 7: Respond promptly to any inspection objections. Don't ghost.
  • Day 15: Check appraisal came in reasonably (ask your agent)
  • Day 20: Confirm lender approval is on track (your agent will tell you)
  • Day 25: Gather receipts for any repairs done. Prepare for final walkthrough.
  • Day 28: Review Closing Disclosure carefully. Verify net proceeds match expectations.
  • Day 30: Go to closing, sign papers, hand over keys. Done.

FAQ

Q: Can the buyer back out after they accept?
A: Yes. Before inspection ends, they can walk away and lose earnest money. After inspection period ends, backing out usually means losing earnest money (unless financing/appraisal issue). Read your contract terms.

Q: Can I back out after accepting?
A: Technically, but you lose earnest money and could be sued for specific performance (buyer sues to force you to sell). Don't accept an offer you don't want.

Q: Who pays for appraisal?
A: Buyer's lender pays. You don't.

Q: What if buyer's lender is slow?
A: Happens often. Delays closing 2–3 days. Communicate early, set expectations. Most lenders clear 30-day closing easily.

Q: Do I have to fix inspection items?
A: No. You can offer credit instead. Negotiate. If you can't agree, deal breaks.
